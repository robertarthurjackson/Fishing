The Debt Wall

How much money has to be created, and when, for the world's debts to keep rolling over — a working model of the refinancing wall, the interest-rate roll-up, and the liquidity growth it forces.

The mechanism

Markets are refinancing systems, not funding systems

Modern capital markets no longer exist primarily to raise new money. On Michael Howell's estimate, roughly three of every four dollars transacted in financial markets are rolling over debts that already exist. With a global debt stock near $350 trillion and an average maturity around seven years, about $50 trillion has to be refinanced every year before a single dollar of new borrowing happens. The model below reproduces his 70–80% figure from those two inputs alone.

The system cannot let the stock default, because the old debt is the collateral under the new money: government bonds back repo, bank balance sheets, money-market funds, and derivative margin. A failed rollover is not one creditor's loss — it is a hole in the collateral pool that every other rollover leans on. So when private balance-sheet capacity can't absorb the wall at prevailing yields, yields rise until something breaks, and then the state's own balance sheet — the central bank — absorbs it anyway. Every stress event of the past decade (repo 2019, COVID 2020, gilts 2022, SVB 2023) ended the same way, within days.

That is the accounting identity this page walks through:

Gross financing need = maturing debt + new deficit.
Buyers = private savings + bank money creation + central-bank money creation.
Whatever private demand won't take at tolerable yields, the monetary system must — so the question is never whether the residual gets monetized, only its size and timing.

The core question

Is monetization a mathematical truth?

Strictly, no single line of algebra forces the printing press — it forces a choice. The governing identity is that the debt ratio stabilizes only when the primary balance covers the interest–growth gap: pb ≥ (r − g) ÷ (1 + g) × debt/GDP. Every escape from the debt spiral has to satisfy that identity from one side or the other, and there are exactly three ways to do it. Using the assumptions you've set above:

The theorem, stated carefully: arithmetic alone doesn't force monetization — it forces one of three exits. Exit 1 requires a permanent fiscal consolidation on the order of a trillion dollars a year, which no US Congress has ever sustained. Exits 2 and 3 both work by holding the growth of money above the return paid to bondholders — nominal growth doing the diluting in one case, capped yields in the other — and a sovereign can only guarantee r < g by having its monetary system absorb the issuance. So the precise claim is: every politically available path runs through monetization; the arithmetic just prices the alternatives. This is also the 1946–1980 American playbook — debt/GDP fell from 106% to under 30% with barely a primary surplus, via capped and negative real yields against fast nominal growth.

Two things make this part of the same story rather than a separate one. First, the trust fund holds only Treasury IOUs: every dollar of "redemption" between now and 2032 is a dollar of new public borrowing, already inside the deficit path above. Second, when depletion arrives, history suggests Congress will not allow a 22% cut to land on 60 million voters — the toggle in the assumptions panel shows what papering over the gap with borrowing does to the issuance wall from FY2033. Medicare's HI fund follows on a similar timeline. These are obligations that, like the bonds, will most plausibly be met in nominal terms with created money, not defaulted on explicitly.

The ceiling

Why the US can't run Japan's playbook

Japan proves a sovereign can carry 250% of GDP without a nominal default. It does not prove the US can — the two systems fail in different places, because the marginal buyer is a different animal.

Reserve-currency status cuts both ways, and this is the thought worth fleshing out. It raises the ceiling in one sense: the world runs structurally short of dollars — trade is invoiced in them, reserves are held in them, and there are roughly $13T of dollar debts owed outside the US that need servicing — so there is a built-in bid for dollar assets no other issuer enjoys, and some of any debasement is exported to foreign holders rather than felt at home. But it lowers the ceiling in the sense that matters here: the US cannot conscript its creditors. Japan's lenders are its own citizens, who cannot leave the yen without leaving Japan; a third of America's lenders are foreigners who hold Treasuries by choice, watch the fiscal arithmetic professionally, and since the 2022 reserve freezes have been quietly swapping marginal reserves into gold — the likeliest engine of gold's repricing. So the binding constraint on the US is not quantity but trust at a price: the debt ratio at which the long end and the dollar revolt is well below 250%, and the revolt arrives as a repricing event, not a gradual squeeze. Which is precisely why the US path leads through monetization earlier in debt/GDP terms than Japan's did — the Fed becomes the captive buyer that Japan grew organically, because nobody else is captive.

Sequencing

The great debasement, in order of operations

Debasement is a ratio trade: debt over nominal GDP. You can shrink the numerator only with surpluses nobody will vote for — or grow the denominator with created money and hope output soaks up enough of it. Read that way, the sequencing hypothesis is coherent: China moved first, easing the yuan and letting its central bank swap reserves into gold, then paused when the currency pressure got uncomfortable — but its property-debt overhang gives it the same arithmetic and little choice but to resume. The US, by this reading, hasn't started: policy at the July-2026 baseline is still nominally tight, real yields are still positive, QT is still (officially) running. The debasement phase for the issuer of the reserve currency has to be prepared, not announced.

That is where the AI buildout fits. A debasement launched into a stagnant economy is just inflation — money grows, output doesn't, prices take the whole adjustment. Launched into an economy with a genuine productivity wave, the same money growth is partly absorbed by real output: g rises, the r < g gap gets easier to engineer, and the inflation that remains is low-grade enough to be politically survivable. The trillion-dollar AI capex cycle is, on this view, the denominator being built in advance — the 2020s equivalent of the postwar boom that made 1946–1980 repression tolerable: debt/GDP fell 80 points while real yields were negative, because the real economy was compounding underneath.

The honest counterpoints belong in the model too. The buildout is itself credit-financed, so it adds private paper to the same wall it is meant to dilute. The timing may not cooperate: the rollover bulge and the OASI depletion date land 2027–2033, while broad AI productivity gains could easily arrive later. A hot economy raises r as well as g, and the foreign third of the Treasury market gets a vote on every auction along the way. Run-it-hot-then-debase is the strategy that makes sense of the pieces — but the wall's timetable, not the strategist's, decides whether the economy is ready when the bill arrives.

Bond-market reaction function

The playbook, in sequence

Digestion

Supply is absorbed but the price of absorption rises: curve bear-steepens, term premium grinds higher, long-end auctions get sloppier. Equities can rally through this stage — liquidity is still growing.

Watch: term premium, bill share, bid-to-cover.

Strain

Private balance sheets saturate. Auction tails repeat, dealer take-downs jump, swap spreads go more negative, MOVE lifts. Officials start talking about "market functioning" and buybacks scale up.

Watch: dealer take, swap spreads, MOVE, buyback sizes.

Break

A plumbing event forces the issue — repo Sep 2019, COVID dash-for-cash Mar 2020 ($1.6T of QE in weeks), UK gilts/LDI Oct 2022 (BoE buying within 48 hours), SVB Mar 2023 (BTFP overnight). The interval from "stress" to "backstop" is measured in days.

Watch: SOFR−IORB, SRF usage, an overnight facility with a new acronym.

Absorption

The state balance sheet takes the wall, one euphemism at a time: QT ends → "reserve management" bill purchases → SLR relief so banks can warehouse bonds → QE proper → and at the limit, yield-curve control. Bonds are capped; the currency and real assets take the adjustment instead.

Watch: Fed balance sheet inflection, SLR rule changes, gold, bitcoin.

The end-state is the point of the whole exercise: nominal default is off the table, because the bonds are the system's collateral — so the adjustment is routed through the unit of account. Bondholders get repaid in full, in money that buys less. The model above is an attempt to put a number and a date range on how much of that money there has to be.
